# Most People Suck at Growth

## Summary

Audrey (18 years old) shares hard-won lessons from being in the "passenger seat of 100 companies bootstrapping to 7-8 figures" and delivering marketing work to 600+ companies. Growth is deceptively simple but brutally hard — it's about doing the thing, tracking numbers religiously, and ultimately being a product person.

## Core Thesis

> "Being good at growth is really, really hard. It is the hardest job in the industry."

Growth is deceptive because it's the simplest role (get paying customers) with a proven playbook, yet most people fail at it. Technical people often can't do growth. Non-technical people think it's their easy way in. Both are wrong.

## The Playbook (Boring but Profitable)

### B2B (No Network/Social Presence)
1. Scrape every email under the sun
2. Validate all emails
3. Cold email all of them
4. Create content-based value pipeline
5. Drip feed leads while building social presence
6. Market repeatedly until they buy or block you

### B2C (Easy Option)
1. Orchestrate organic content farm
2. Post across hundreds/thousands of channels
3. Turn organic content into ads (paid scales)

**For both:** Everybody funnels to email — "the most powerful channel in the world."

## The Two Deceptions

### Deception 1: Fame ≠ Growth
> "You would not know 99% of the companies that I worked with. But these companies generated millions, served customers well, and grew month after month."

- Large marketing/growth hires are often famous people or content creators
- You may think you need fame or content skills to nail growth
- **Reality:** Going viral is a positive signal, not a qualification
- You can be famous and totally incompetent at growing a company
- Being famous is a plus "in the same way that going to Harvard might be 'impressive'"

### Deception 2: Growth is Glamorous
> "If you hear a growth strategy that is difficult but not clever, it is generally a bad strategy. If you hear a growth strategy that is flashy for the sake of flashy, you are likely falling into the pitfall."

- People who haven't done growth over-complicate it
- Growth is over-complicated and over-glamorized
- **Best way to get good at growth:** Not by studying content creators, growth hacks, or public-facing marketers

## The Framework: Two Things to Do

### 1. Be Incredibly Principled

**The only thing that matters:** Getting users/customers/clients.

**There is no in-between:**
- Planning to do the thing ≠ doing the thing
- Thinking about the thing ≠ doing the thing
- Delegating someone to do the thing ≠ doing the thing
- **Only doing the thing = doing the thing**

**Success definition:** Money up. No money = no success.
- Sales calls ≠ success (unless closed with money in account)
- Waitlist ≠ success
- Pipeline ≠ success
- Replies ≠ success
- Views ≠ success
- **Fundraising ≠ success**

> "If the customer dashboard is not spiking upwards, your growth is a failure."

**Track numbers religiously:**
- Growth is not "post and hope" or "I will email people"
- It's pulling levers at scale orders of magnitude larger than imagined
- Need adequate sample size to understand what works
- Build funnel in reverse to know exactly where next $100K is coming from

**Volume math:**
- Assuming 10% conversion at each funnel stage
- You can reach 1,000+ people per day with strong intent
- Can condense this loop into one day
- B2B: Set up 20 inboxes, send 500 emails from each daily (inefficient but profitable)
- **Efficiency doesn't matter if you're doing the thing and numbers go up**

> "There is no reason you cannot multiply your current weekly revenue by 10."

### 2. Be a Product Person

**The hard truth:**
> "The best growth people are product people. Growth is product."

**The lever analogy:**
- Product pulls the far end of a lever (longer moment arm)
- Same result (lever moving X inches) requires less force
- Great product makes great growth easy
- Product moves you up the lever, determines where you pull growth from

**Why content creators don't make the cut:**
- Growth is understanding the customer and market
- Takes specific product mind to see non-obvious desire signs
- Need deeply defined taste when executing product vision
- Need conviction to not get distracted

**Product people can:**
- See angles nobody else sees
- Extrapolate angles everybody wants but nobody articulates
- Make observations others can't AND take action on them

**Signs of product people:**
- Product/service is buttery smooth
- Immensely intuitive
- Immediately valuable
- Rare experience where you feel good when you just expected baseline
- "Cannot be faked, painfully obvious when somebody tries"

**What it takes:**
- Relentless personal study
- Incredibly open listening
- Vision for world bigger than average person can see

> "People that can do this have magic in their fingertips. They have a center of gravity a few steps into the future."

## The Good News

**Growth can lead you to product:**
- Being good at product makes you good at growth
- Being good at growth will make you good at product
- While you can't force visionary talent, you can take steps that land you where talent is

**How:**
- Talking to people (really talking)
- Actually trying to sell them stuff
- Figuring out what people want and don't want
- Implementing changes based on feedback
- Being profitable
- All manually, at scales larger than imaginable

**The path:**
> "It will lead you to places you have yet to see."

## On AI and Growth

> "I enjoy doing growth by hand. I just think it's better, because humans can understand humans on a level that AI can't, no matter how much they pretend."

- Yes, this is a "boomer take"
- May change mind down the line
- But: humans are using your product, so you need to be that human

## The Final Irony

> "After all my talk about numbers and strategy, growth is not really about growth."

Growth is about product. Product is about vision. Vision is about seeing what others can't.

A collection of thoughts for those who want to get into early-stage (<8-figures profit), grow their own skillset or company, and be really good at what they do.

Being good at growth is really, really hard. It is the hardest job in the industry. There are a lot of technical people I've met in my career that simply cannot do growth. They usually either need to find a guy, or go through an arduous internal reckoning. And on the flip side, the non-technical folks who want to get into tech or business think that growth is their easy way in.

I should clarify this is aimed at nobody in particular. I suspect this is because growth is deceptive. It is the simplest role: get paying customers. And get a lot of them. The playbook is proven, too. There's no deep research, there's no technical hurdle arising from venturing into unknown territory, and if you're effective, you don't even need to manage any team members until you're at a few million in revenue, minimum.

There are a set number of go-to-market strategies, and of that selection, there are a few options that work. I'll give you two examples.

If you're B2B with no network/social presence, you should scrape every email under the sun, validate all the emails, cold email all of them, create a content-based value pipeline that you drip feed said leads while building a social presence, and then market over and over to them until they buy or block you.

If you're in B2C, there are a few more options, but the easy one I can think of is that you orchestrate an organic content farm and post across hundreds, if not thousands, of channels. You turn all your organic content into ads because paid methodologies scale.

For both B2B and B2C, everybody is funneled to an email list because the fact of growth is that email is the most powerful channel in the world, so if you want to get into growth, you should get good at emailing. I might sound a little outdated, but this is my experience. And you can get creative, too. This is the boring stuff, but boring is profitable. Plus, you optimize everything from there.

By the way, I am sure that you've heard this before. I'm not saying anything new. But it is difficult because of how simple it is.

In my previous company, I built growth infrastructure and consulted on marketing and sales for service companies. I've been in what I like to call the passenger seat of 100 companies bootstrapping to 7-8 figures, and my team and I delivered marketing work to over 600 companies.

None of this is to brag. I'm 18 years old, so my experience is limited, and bootstrapped numbers always look small in comparison to the venture ecosystem that we're in. But I think my experience has given me a core observation: what growth looks like to the world is rarely the growth that moves the needle.

The first relieving factor is that how famous you are, or how famous a company is, within certain boundaries, has absolutely zero relevance to how much revenue you or the company can generate. You would not know 99% of the companies that I worked with. But these companies generated millions, served customers well, and grew month after month and year after year.

This is the first core deception. A large amount of marketing and growth hires nowadays are famous people, or were previously content creators. So you may think that you need to be famous, or get good at content, in order to nail growth. Or, you may think that content is growth.

I should mention that all of my friends that are large content creators in the space happen to be good at growth, so this is not a diss at anybody, but more of a reassurance to anybody without a large presence: going viral or being famous on social media is a positive signal, but not a qualification for a growth role. You can be famous, and be totally incompetent at growing a company. This is the majority.

Unless your content is one-to-one with the niche you're in, being famous or being a content creator is a plus for growth roles in the same way that going to Harvard might be "impressive". It doesn't really mean anything when you scrutinize it at the level that it needs to be scrutinized.

Growth is over-complicated and over-glamorized. If you hear a growth strategy that is difficult but not clever, it is generally a bad strategy. If you hear a growth strategy that is flashy for the sake of flashy, you are likely falling into the pitfall number two of glamorization.

People that have not done growth before, but think they know growth, will always over-complicate the process. But growth is not complicated. The best way to get good at growth is not by studying content creators, or by studying growth hacks, or by following the footsteps of really anybody public-facing in marketing, including me. Which means you should take everything here with a grain of salt.

Now that you've consumed the salt, there are two things you should do if you want to be a strong early-stage operator, or someone with a real knack for growth. It is simple, in fact, almost annoyingly simple. But it is a framework that has guided me well in my marketing work with companies around the world.

The first is boring, and that is to be incredibly principled.

Sounds like baloney. But when you are running growth, there is only one thing that matters: getting users. Or customers. Or clients. This is "the thing". We know this.

From here, there are two sides. There is no in-between. You are either doing the thing, or you are not doing the thing. Planning to do the thing is not doing the thing. Thinking about the thing is not doing the thing. Delegating someone to do the thing is not doing the thing. The only thing that is doing the thing is doing the thing.

The reason why I mention principled is because if you actually want to be successful in growth, the number needs to go up. And if it doesn't go up, you are failing. Again, this is all stupidly, stupidly obvious. But you will not believe the amount of people that work on tangents, get distracted, build in spaces of their company that aren't "doing the thing". It is almost as if they hate money. It is painful to see.

If you have no revenue, or your revenue is not constantly increasing beyond your expectations (yes, beyond), you have not put enough effort into growth, which means that you have not helped enough people.

If the first principle is to do the thing, the second principle should define success and failure for said thing, assuming you are doing it. Money up is success. There is no success if there is no money. This is not because money is all-important. Money is just physical proof that you have something that somebody wants.

Sales calls are not a success unless closed. Closed as in the money is in your account. A waitlist is definitely not success, and neither is pipeline, nor replies, nor views. Especially important: fundraising is not success. If the customer dashboard is not spiking upwards, your growth is a failure.

The third principle derives itself from the second principle, where if you understand success and failure, you can understand how to tilt yourself in the direction of success. Which means, you need to be able to track your numbers religiously.

Growth is not a "post and hope it works" endeavor, or an "I will email people" endeavor. It is pulling levers at a scale orders of magnitude larger than you imagined if you want to sprint your user acquisition as fast as possible. To achieve this, you need to know your numbers better than you know anything else.

Perhaps you are actually quite skilled at this marketing and sales stuff, but your volume is lacking. Or, you are not skilled, but volume will save you. One of these two scenarios is likely the case for many people.

If you're not constantly going after 1,000+ people with strong intent, either your market is not large enough or you haven't gone hard enough. This loop can be condensed into one day. In other words, you can reach far more than a thousand people per day. There is no reason you can't.

This is a limiting belief for a lot of people in growth. A lot of people have never worked on something one million times bigger than themselves, and their own incapacity to comprehend the wonders that await them accidentally handicaps their ability to output great go-to-market.

If you knew that your growth would work, you would do nothing but growth. If you knew that 100,000 new users were one strong ad campaign away, you would launch ads every day until you hit the winner. And you would make them significantly faster than you thought you could. Even if the first 100 flopped.

Or, for example, in B2B, you can easily set up multiple inboxes (I would recommend 20) and send at least 500 emails from every single inbox every single day. And that's objectively the most inefficient, highly profitable way of doing it, and you can still establish a proper funnel and understand your conversion metrics to a tee by doing this.

Because as long as you are doing the thing and the number goes up, efficiency doesn't really matter. The adage of doing things that don't scale is very true.

Assuming you are at any number above zero, barring obvious exceptions, there is no reason you cannot multiply your current weekly revenue by 10. There is no objection. If you don't know how, you either haven't figured it out or don't believe you can.

You need an adequate enough sample size to understand what is working and what is not working. You need to be able to build out your funnel in reverse to know exactly where your next $100,000 is coming from.

Assuming a 10% conversion rate in each stage of the funnel, you can quickly understand what your volume and strategy needs to be, and what areas of the business need to be worked on. When you know these numbers, you just follow your "trophic level"-esque blueprint and work your way up to understand how the next customers are entering your world.

Growth is a game of proving and disproving hypotheses. People will have different philosophies on this, but for me, growth is exceedingly mathematical. It is a numbers game, and it is a numbers game that you can win. The only thinking you do is thinking that optimizes the numbers. All creativity flows from the numbers, not the other way around.

I'm a creative soul at heart: I grew up painting every week, and also loved the piano and the cello (I still do). But when I am in growth mode, I am doing numerical analysis, and nothing else. People make growth sound really creative. I don't think it is. Creativity only comes in when necessary, and many times, it's not necessary. Probably disappointing to hear. But it is fun in its own way.

People ask if AI can help with growth and marketing. It is likely worth mentioning that I enjoy doing growth by hand. I just think it's better, because humans can understand humans on a level that AI can't, no matter how much they pretend. Yes, this is a boomer take. I may change my mind down the line. Because humans are using your product, if you want to be good at growth, you need to be that human.

And, in that way, it's kind of funny. Because after all my talk about numbers and strategy, growth is not really about growth.

And this brings me to the second "thing" you should do if you want to be a skilled, early-stage operator.

The reason why growth is so fundamentally difficult is because the best growth people are product people. Growth is product. But why?

The easy answer would be that a great product with terrible growth outperforms a terrible product with great growth. But in the current landscape, that wouldn't really be true.

It's much more effective to think of product vs growth in the way you might think of torque. Product pulls a far end of a lever, which has a longer moment arm. Which means, to achieve the same result (lever moving X inches), you need less force to produce the same rotational effect.

In that sense, the product moves you up the lever, and that determines where you can pull growth from. That is, a great product makes great growth easy.

Making the bold assumption that working on the product will lead you into a direction of more users, the better way to get more users is to make the product better, because that will make more users come more easily.

And I'm not necessarily talking about this from a technical perspective. Because if product-market fit is the hardest thing for a company, then the foundational endeavor of a company needs to be achieving that fit. Because if the company has no fit, the company is default dead, which means there is no growth.

Growth is difficult because of how easy it seems. As I said in the beginning, deceptively so. But, when you contextualize growth in the realm of product, suddenly it starts to make more sense.

An operator's job is not just about getting more users. It's about getting the product to a place where users want it.

That is why content creators don't necessarily make the cut for full-time growth hires. Or why all the growth advice you will hear is missing a fundamental component. Including my growth advice above.

Because growth is understanding the customer. And understanding the market. And it takes a specific product mind to do that. To be able to look at desire signs that are not immediately obvious, to have deeply defined taste when executing a product vision, and to have enough conviction to not get distracted along the way.

Product people are rare because they can see the angle that nobody else sees. Or, they can extrapolate the angle that everybody wants but nobody articulates properly.

True growth people are product people at heart, and true product people are visionaries. I almost feel like I am not doing them enough justice in my description. It is like an "idea guy" on steroids, where they can make observations that others can't, and actually take action on said ideas.

There is a reason why Jobs is one-of-a-kind, and why people don't just refer to him as the "nontechnical growth guy" (well, maybe somebody does). It's because he architected the future of personal computing, made bold bets and suffered for them, understood what people wanted, and delivered.

Product people also transcend the technical and non-technical barrier. It's not about coding the product, or just about talking to people and taking sales calls. It's a deep understanding of something the world needs, and being able to tug that string to execute something phenomenal.

The signs are obvious, too: their product or service will always be buttery smooth, immensely intuitive, immediately valuable, and is one of those rare experiences where you feel good when you just expected a baseline.

It is not something that can be faked, and it's painfully obvious when somebody tries. It is a culmination of relentless personal study, incredibly open listening, and a vision for the world that is bigger than the average person can see.

And most people cannot do this, including me. People that can do this have magic in their fingertips. They have a center of gravity a few steps into the future. And I admire them for it.

But enough gushing. The good news here is that growth can lead you to product. While being good at product makes you good at growth, being good at growth will make you good at product.

Because while you can't force a visionary-like talent, you can take steps that land you where the talent is. Talking to people. Really talking to people, and actually trying to sell them stuff. Figuring out what people want and don't want. Implementing changes based on what those people say. Being profitable. All manually, at scales larger than imaginable.

It will lead you to places you have yet to see. All these skills are learned. However, I don't think it can be taught. Not even in written like this, or writing much better than this.

If something can be learned but not taught, the only solution is to do it. Go out there. Be principled. Do the thing.
