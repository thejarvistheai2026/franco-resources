# Wide Moat Investing: The Morningstar Framework

## Summary

An in-depth explanation of Morningstar's economic moat framework for identifying companies with sustainable competitive advantages. The article breaks down the five sources of moats and how to apply this framework to investment decisions.

## Key Concepts

### What Is an Economic Moat?

An economic moat is a structural competitive advantage that protects a company from competition and allows it to earn excess returns on capital for an extended period.

### The Five Sources of Moats

1. **Network Effect** — Value increases as more users join (e.g., Visa, Mastercard)
2. **Intangible Assets** — Brands, patents, regulatory licenses (e.g., Disney, Coca-Cola)
3. **Cost Advantage** — Lower cost structure than competitors (e.g., Walmart)
4. **Switching Costs** — High cost for customers to change providers (e.g., SAP, Oracle)
5. **Efficient Scale** — Limited market size deters new entrants (e.g., utilities)

### Moat Width

- **Wide Moat** — Competitive advantage expected to last 20+ years
- **Narrow Moat** — Advantage expected to last 10-20 years
- **No Moat** — No sustainable competitive advantage

## Key Insights

- Moats protect against competition, not disruption
- The best companies often have multiple moat sources
- Moat durability matters more than moat width
- Price matters: even great companies can be bad investments at the wrong price
