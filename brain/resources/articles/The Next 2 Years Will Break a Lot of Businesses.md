# The Next 2 Years Will Break a Lot of Businesses

## Summary

Jackson Blackledge argues that agency and e-commerce business models have a 2-year shelf life due to AI disruption. The cost structure of the industry is being rewritten, and founders who don't adapt fast enough will see their models become irrelevant while competitors pick up their clients.

## Key Thesis

> "The model most of you are running right now has a 2-year shelf life. maybe less. not because the market dried up. Not because you're doing something wrong. because the entire cost structure of this industry is about to get rewritten."

## The 6 Shifts

### 1. AI Ads Become Indistinguishable from Human Ones
- Quality gap between AI and human creative has closed faster than expected
- AI ads now performing better than human creatives on many metrics
- **Implication:** Brands paying $5K-$15K/month for creative production will find comparable output for fraction of the cost
- **Survival factor:** Agencies must sell *thinking* (strategy, positioning) not *production*

### 2. Content Becomes a Commodity
- Unlimited AI content at zero marginal cost = volume stops being an advantage
- **What cuts through:** Trust, not more posts
- **For ecom:** Distribution through micro-influencers, community, strategic sponsorships
- **For agencies:** Personal brand becomes the differentiator when all agencies run same tools

### 3. Low-Level Execution Gets Automated
- Elon cut Twitter from 8,000 to 2,000 people — company ran fine
- Repeatable, process-driven work is automatable now or within 12 months
- **Two types survive:**
  - Specialists doing work AI can't replicate
  - Operators who know how to point AI at problems and get results
- **Prediction:** One strategist with right agent stack could replace entire team of media buyers

### 4. Agencies That Can't Prove ROI Disappear
- Clients losing patience for retainers tied to *activity* (emails sent, content generated)
- **New question:** "What actually changed in my business because of what you did?"
- **Winners:** Agencies who build model around delivering clear outcomes
- **Losers:** Agencies still selling deliverables and process

### 5. LLM Chatbots Become a Serious Acquisition Channel
- Ads coming to ChatGPT — potentially bigger than Facebook 2013 or TikTok 2020
- **Data advantage:** People dump thoughts/problems/desires to models daily — 10x what Meta had
- **Google AI Mode:** Checkout built right in, products placed directly in AI responses
- **Action items:**
  - Clean product feed (AI pulls from Merchant Center)
  - Optimize for conversational queries, not just keywords
  - Stack reviews/social proof (AI weighs trust signals)
  - Build landing pages for research-stage queries
  - Test PMax and Demand Gen campaigns

### 6. Ecom Brands Forced to Fix Unit Economics
- Low CPM "hacks" and thin margins are under pressure
- **New threat:** Suppliers can now become brands using open-source models (Kimi K2.5, etc.)
  - Produce creative, build landing pages, test offers at pace/cost brands can't match
  - Can spin up 100 stores, run volume at scale
  - Willing to undercut because they own supply chain
- **Survival metrics:** AOV and LTV must work at current CAC — costs aren't coming down
- **Growth levers:** Branding, community, subscriptions, email flows, loyalty (not afterthoughts)

The next 2 years are going to break a lot of businesses

i'm going to say something that's going to upset a lot of agency owners and ecom founders. the model most of you are running right now has a 2-year shelf life. maybe less. not because the market dried up. Not because you're doing something wrong. because the entire cost structure of this industry is about to get rewritten, and most people are treating it like a slow-moving trend they can deal with later. later is the wrong answer. i've watched founders with genuinely solid businesses get wiped out by changes they saw coming and just didn't move on fast enough. and the cost of being late here is your whole model becoming irrelevant while someone who moved six months earlier is picking up your clients. so here's my honest read on where things are going and what it means if you're running an agency or an ecom brand right now.

**1. ai ads become indistinguishable from human ones**

over the last 12 months, the quality gap between ai-generated creative and human-made creative has closed faster than anyone in this industry was prepared for. back then, you could tell immediately if something was made with ai. now I'm seeing ads where I genuinely can't tell. the models keep improving every few months, and whatever gap is left probably will close within the next year or two. we're seeing it in our own client accounts. for this brand, the 2 ai ads we created are stacking up well against human creatives. they're actually performing better on many metrics.

what that does to ad agencies is pretty serious. the whole reason brands paid agencies and freelancers to produce ads, scripts, emails, and landing pages was the assumption that good creative was hard to make and required real human skill. that assumption doesn't hold the way it used to. brands paying $5,000 to $15,000 a month for creative production are going to figure out they can get comparable output for a fraction of that. and when they do, the agencies built entirely around production volume are going to feel it immediately. the ones who get through this are the ones who already understand that the creative was never actually the product. the thinking behind it was. the person who figured out what to say, who to say it to, and why it would convert. that's what drove results.

**2. content becomes a commodity**

a lot of channels are about to get buried with ai generated content. when ai can produce unlimited content at zero marginal cost, volume stops being an advantage. more posts, more ads, more everything, you're just adding to the noise. what cuts through is trust. for ecom brands that means distribution through people your customers already believe. micro-influencers with loyal audiences, strategic sponsorships, a community that buys because of who you are. that's an acquisition channel that compounds in a way paid ads alone never will. for agency owners, your version of that is your personal brand. when every agency runs the same tools and produces the same deliverables, the only reason a client picks you is because they trust you and your company specifically. the longer you wait to build that, the harder it gets. the noise keeps compounding, and the people who started six months ago are that much harder to catch.

**3. low-level execution gets almost entirely automated**

when elon took over twitter, one of the first things he did was cut the team from 8,000 to under 2,000. the company didn't collapse. in most areas it ran fine. what that showed is that a huge chunk of those roles existed just to manage process, not move anything forward. ai is about to make that true for every business. the repeatable, process-driven work that entire teams were built around is either already automatable or will be within the next 12 months. the middle layer of generalists and low-level executors gets compressed. one person with the right systems can now do what 3-5 people were doing before. what's left are two types of people. specialists doing work ai genuinely can't replicate, and operators who know how to point ai at a problem and get results. that's what every high-performing team is going to be built around. and if ai agents keep improving, it's not a stretch to say a single strategist with the right agent stack could replace an entire team of low-level media buyers the operators who get ahead of this and start operating at that level early are going to be worth a lot. the ones still positioning themselves as low-level executors are going to find that market getting smaller every year.

**4. agencies that can't prove roi disappear**

clients have been patient about paying retainers tied to activity for a long time. that patience is running out. agencies got away with charging for deliverables for years. emails sent. content generated. account management. clients paid partly because the results were hard to trace directly, and partly because switching was enough of a pain that they stuck around anyway. that's shifting. as ai brings execution costs down and attribution gets more accurate, clients are going to have a lot less tolerance for paying for work that can't show a clear line to revenue, profit, or growth. the question agencies are going to hear more and more: "what actually changed in my business because of what you did?" the agencies that can answer that with real specifics, and build their whole model around delivering those outcomes, are going to get stronger as things tighten up. the ones still selling on deliverables and process are going to lose those clients either to agencies that can make the roi case, or to ai tools that cost less.

**5. llm chatbots become a serious acquisition channel**

you've probably heard ads are coming to chatgpt. and honestly, I don't think people realize how big this is gonna be for ecom seen guys saying it's facebook in 2013 or tiktok in 2020. it might get even bigger than that. people are dumping their thoughts to these models every single day. problems, frustrations, desires, plans, etc. all in their own words. the volume and depth of that data is going to be 10x what meta ever had. that should make their targeting way more precise. plus the level of personalization you can unlock is on a different tier. google's also acting fast on this. we're watching it happen in ai mode with checkout built right in. your product gets placed directly in the ai's response.

now, in case you're wondering how this affects google search, i don't think it'll change much. people have been calling for the death of google since chatgpt became popular a few years ago. meanwhile google ad clicks just hit its 5-year high in q4 last year. the game will be about learning how to: - take advantage of these new channels - run it alongside existing channels to complement each other here's where i'd do on top of your normal google strategy to prepare for this shift: make sure your product feed is clean. ai pulls from merchant center data. optimise product titles and descriptions for conversational queries, not just keywords stack reviews and social proof. ai systems weigh trust signals heavily start building landing pages for informational and research-stage queries test pmax and demand gen campaigns. these are likely the first campaign types to serve in AI Mode

**6. ecom brands are forced to fix their unit economics**

the brands that built on low cpms, "hacks", and thin margins are under massive pressure. acquisition costs keep going up. ios changes broke the attribution models everyone was running on. and ai-generated creative flooding paid channels means more competition and higher costs on top of that another issue is that suppliers can now easily step into the role of a brand with access to open-source models like kimi k2.5 (developed with serious compute backing in china), they can produce ad creative, build landing pages, and test offers at a pace and cost that most ecom brands can't match. there's nothing stopping them from plugging in agents, spinning up 100 stores, and running volume at scale. on top of that, they're willing to undercut on price because they own the supply chain. a brand with weak unit economics has nowhere to run in that environment. if your aov is too low and your ltv doesn't cover your cac, you can't spend your way to profitability and you can't optimize your way out of a model that's broken at the foundation. the brands coming out of this in a strong position are the ones who treat aov and ltv as core business metrics. that means taking branding, community, subscriptions, email flows, and loyalty seriously as growth levers, not afterthoughts. the unit economics need to work at what acquisition costs right now, because there's no real reason to think those costs are coming down.

— i'm not writing this to stress anyone out. i'm writing it because i spent years making expensive mistakes i could have avoided if someone had laid this out before it was obvious. none of this needs perfect information. it needs honesty about what you're already seeing and a willingness to move before it feels completely safe to move. the people who do that are going to look like they saw it coming. the ones who wait are going to be asking themselves how it all shifted so fast.
