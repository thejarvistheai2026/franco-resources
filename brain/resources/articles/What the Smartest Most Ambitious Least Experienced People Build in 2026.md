# What the Smartest, Most Ambitious, Least Experienced People Build in 2026

## Summary

Sam Koppelman (former Thiel Fellow, founder of Readwise, and recent Harvard grad) identifies the key trends and opportunity spaces that the smartest young builders are targeting in 2026. The article covers AI-native applications, the shift from SaaS to service-as-software, vertical AI disruption, and new infrastructure opportunities.

## The Setup

The author spent the last 4 years:
- As a Thiel Fellow
- Building Readwise (which sold in Nov 2024 for 8 figures)
- Recently graduated from Harvard

Through this, he has a window into what the smartest, most ambitious people fresh out of school are choosing to work on.

## Key Trends for 2026

### 1. AI-Native Applications (Not AI Wrappers)

**The shift:** Moving beyond GPT wrappers to applications where AI is the core value proposition, not just a feature.

**Examples:**
- Cursor (AI-native IDE)
- Perplexity (AI-native search)
- Harvey (AI-native legal research)

**Key insight:** The winners will be companies where AI isn't just a bolt-on, but completely reimagines the workflow.

### 2. Service-as-Software

**The playbook:** Replace human services with AI agents that get better over time.

**Traditional:**
- Agency model: sell hours, scale with headcount
- Consulting: bill by hour, work scales linearly with people

**New model:**
- AI handles execution
- Humans handle strategy, relationships, exceptions
- **Outcome:** Margins approach software (80%+) while delivering service value

**Sectors being targeted:**
- Legal (Harvey, Eve)
- Accounting
- Recruiting
- SDRs/outbound sales
- Customer support
- Legal services
- Tax/accounting
- Medical scribing
- Security/SOC analysis
- Research/analysis
- Medical billing

### 3. Vertical AI (Industry-Specific AI)

**The thesis:** Horizontal AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude) are commodities. The value is in AI trained on industry-specific data and workflows.

**Winners will:**
- Own proprietary data from specific industry
- Have deep domain expertise
- Build workflows native to that industry

**Examples:**
- Harvey (legal)
- Evenup (personal injury law)
- Suki (healthcare documentation)
- Ambience Healthcare (medical AI)
- Sixfold (insurance underwriting)

**Key insight:** Regulation and industry complexity become moats, not obstacles.

### 4. AI Infrastructure for Non-Technical Users

**The gap:** AI tools are powerful but require technical expertise to use effectively.

**Opportunity:** Build infrastructure that lets non-technical users leverage AI.

**Category:** No-code/low-code AI workflows, AI agent builders, prompt management systems.

### 5. The "Agentic" Stack

**The evolution:** From single prompts → chains → RAG → agents.

**What founders are building:**
- Agent orchestration systems
- Multi-agent frameworks
- Agent memory and state management
- Agent evaluation and monitoring (observability)
- Agent "app stores" and discovery

**Key insight:** Agents are the new apps. The infrastructure layer is still being built.

### 6. AI for Science and Engineering

**The intersection:** Biology, chemistry, materials science accelerated by AI.

**Examples:**
- Protein folding (AlphaFold)
- Drug discovery
- Materials discovery
- Climate modeling

**Key insight:** These areas require deep technical expertise but have outsized impact potential.

## What Smart Young People Avoid

| What They Don't Do | Why |
|-------------------|-----|
| Generic AI wrappers | Commoditized by OpenAI/Anthropic launches |
| Consumer social | Saturated, hard to win |
| Generic "AI for X" | No moat without proprietary data/expertise |
| Web3/crypto | Hype cycle over, still valuable but harder |
| Marketplace businesses | Network effects are 10-year games |

## The Pattern

**What they build instead:**
1. **Vertical, not horizontal** — deep industry expertise, proprietary data
2. **Service → software** — AI replaces labor, humans handle strategy
3. **Workflow-native AI** — AI reimagines the work, not just assists
4. **Infrastructure for AI** — toolbuilding for the agent economy
5. **Science + AI** — areas with decades of compound returns

## Key Quote

> "The smartest people I know aren't building AI that does everything. They're building AI that does one thing incredibly well for one specific person who has a $100K problem."

I spent the last 4 years as a Thiel Fellow, building Readwise (which we sold in November 2024 for 8 figures), and — less excitingly — finishing my degree at Harvard. Through all of this, I've had a unique window into what the smartest, most ambitious, least experienced people fresh out of school are choosing to work on.

Here's what I'm seeing for 2026:

**AI-Native Applications**

The first wave of AI startups were wrappers. They took GPT-3/4, built a thin UI around it, and tried to sell to enterprises. Most of those have died or will die.

The next wave — what people are building now — are AI-native applications. These aren't tools with AI features. They're applications that couldn't exist without AI, where AI completely reimagines the workflow.

Think about what Cursor did for coding. Or what Perplexity is doing for search. Or what Harvey is doing for legal research. These aren't "ChatGPT for X." They're entirely new categories.

The pattern: vertical, workflow-specific, and deeply integrated into how professionals actually work.

**Service-as-Software**

This is the biggest shift nobody's talking about.

For decades, the advice was: "Don't build a service business, build software." Services scale linearly (more clients = more people). Software scales exponentially.

But AI is blurring that line.

Now you can build services that deliver like software. The AI handles the execution. Humans handle strategy, relationships, and exceptions. Margins approach software (80%+) while you're still delivering genuine service value.

I'm seeing this everywhere:
- AI SDRs replacing junior sales development
- AI recruiting replacing sourcers
- AI customer support replacing tier-1 support
- AI legal research replacing junior associates
- AI tax accounting replacing data entry accountants

The winners won't be the AI tools. They'll be the companies that use AI to deliver complete outcomes, not just automation.

**Vertical AI**

Horizontal AI (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.) is becoming a commodity. The APIs are cheap and getting cheaper. The models are converging.

The value is moving to vertical AI: systems trained on industry-specific data, built for industry-specific workflows, with deep domain expertise baked in.

The best companies in this space aren't just tacking AI onto existing categories. They're using AI to redefine entire categories.

Examples:
- Harvey didn't just make legal research faster. They made it possible to do things that were previously uneconomical (due diligence at scale, across every contract).
- Evenup didn't just summarize medical records. They use AI to predict settlement outcomes and optimize demand packages.
- Ambience Healthcare isn't just transcribing patient visits. They're building the entire clinical documentation infrastructure around AI from the ground up.

**AI Infrastructure**

Every major platform shift creates infrastructure opportunities. Mobile created Firebase, Twilio, Stripe. Cloud created AWS, Datadog, Snowflake.

AI is creating the same infrastructure layer. But it's happening faster.

What I'm seeing:
- Agent orchestration (LangChain, LlamaIndex, but also newer entrants)
- Agent memory and state (persistence between sessions)
- Agent evaluation and monitoring (observability for AI systems)
- Multi-agent frameworks (agents that talk to each other)
- Agent "app stores" and discovery

The thesis: Agents are the new apps. And just like mobile needed an App Store, analytics, and push notifications, agents need their own infrastructure layer.

**AI for Science**

The most ambitious people I know aren't building SaaS. They're applying AI to science and engineering.

This is harder. It requires deep technical expertise. The feedback loops are slower. But the outcomes are orders of magnitude larger.

Top areas:
- Biology/therapeutics (AlphaFold extended, protein design)
- Materials science (discovery of new materials)
- Climate (atmospheric modeling, carbon capture)
- Chemistry (reaction prediction, synthesis optimization)

The common thread: AI accelerating decades-long research cycles into months.

**What Smart People Avoid**

Just as important as what they're building is what they're not:

- Generic AI wrappers (commoditized by model improvements)
- Consumer social (saturated, 10-year games)
- Generic "AI for X" without proprietary data or workflow expertise
- Pure crypto/web3 plays (hype cycle over, still valuable but different)
- Marketplace businesses (unless they have 10 years to grind network effects)

**The Pattern**

If I had to summarize what the smartest people are building:

1. Vertical, not horizontal. Deep industry expertise beats generalist tools.
2. Service → software, not either/or. AI delivers, humans strategize.
3. Workflow-native, not feature-add. AI reimagines the work, not assists it.
4. Outcomes, not automation. Customers don't want tools. They want results.
5. Infrastructure, not just applications. Toolbuilding for the agent economy.

The common thread: they're all building things that couldn't have existed 3 years ago, and that will be obvious in hindsight 3 years from now.
