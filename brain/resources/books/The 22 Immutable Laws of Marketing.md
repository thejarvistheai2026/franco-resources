#### Key Details

1. The Law of Leadership It's better to be first than it is to be better. ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 2

- The basic issue in marketing is creating a category you can be first in. It's the law of leadership: It's better to be first than it is to be better." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 3

- "The law of leadership applies to any product, any brand, any category." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 5

- "One reason the first brand tends to maintain its leadership is that the name often becomes generic." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 6

- "If the secret of success is getting into the prospect's mind first, what strategy are most companies committed to? The better-product strategy." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 8

- "Marketing is a battle of perceptions, not products." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 8

- "2. The Law of Category If you can't be first in a category, set up a new category you can be first in." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 10

- "If you didn't get into the prospect's mind first, don't give up hope. Find a new category you can be first in." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 11

- "When you launch a new product, the first question to ask yourself is not "How is this new product better than the competition?" but "what category is this new product first in?"


[…] This is counter to classic marketing thinking, which is brand oriented: How do I get people to prefer my brand? Forget the brand. Think categories. Prospects are on the defensive when it comes to brands.

[..]When you're the first in in a new category, promote the category. In essence, you have no competition." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 13

- "3. The Law of the Mind It's better to be first in the mind than to be first in the marketplace." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 14

- "It's better to be first in the prospect's mind than first in the marketplace. Which, if anything, understates the importance of being first in the mind. Being first in the mind is everything in marketing. Being first in the marketplace is important only to the extent that it allows you to get in the mind first." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 15

- "The law of the mind follows from the law of perception. If marketing is a battle of perception, not product, then the mind takes precedence over the marketplace." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 15

- "The conventional solution to the problem is money. That is, the resources to design and build product or service organizations plus the resources to hold press conferences, attend trade shows, run advertisements, and conduct direct mail programs (chapter 22: The Law of Resources). Unfortunately, this gives rise to the perception that the answer to all marketing questions is the same: money. Not true. More money is wasted in marketing than any other human activity" ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 16

- "4. The Law of Perception Marketing is not a battle of products, it's a battle of perceptions." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 18

- "It's an illusion. There is no objective reality. There are no facts. There are no best products. All that exists in the world of marketing are perceptions in the minds of the customer or prospect. The perception is the reality. Everything else is an illusion." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 19


- "Only by studying how perceptions are formed in the mind and focusing your marketing programs on those perceptions can you overcome your basically incorrect marketing instincts." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 20

- "It's easier to see the power of perception over product when the products are separated by some distance. (Example Honda in USA vs Japan)" ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 22

- "Marketing is a battle of perceptions, not products. Marketing is the process of dealing with those perceptions." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 23

- "What makes the battle even more difficult is that customers frequently make buying decisions based on second-hand perceptions. Instead of using their own perceptions, they base their buying decisions on someone else's perception of reality. This is the "everybody knows" principle." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 24

- "5. The Law of Focus The most powerful concept in marketing is owning a word in the prospect's mind." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 26

- "A company can become incredibly successful if it can find a way to own a word in the mind of the prospect. Not a complicated word. Not an invented one. The simple words are best, words taken right out of the dictionary. This is the law of focus." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 27

- "Federal Express was able to put the word overnight into the minds of its prospects because it sacrificed its product line and focused on overnight package delivery only.

In a way, the law of leadership-it's better to be first than to be better-enables the first brand or company to own a word in the mind of the prospect. But the word the leader owns is so simple that it's invisible." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 27

- "The most effective words are simple and benefit oriented. No matter how complicated the product, no matter how complicated the needs of the market, it's always better to focus on one word or benefit rather than two or three or four." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 28

- "The essence of marketing is narrowing the focus. You become stronger when you reduce the scope of your operations. You can't stand for something if you chase after everything." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 31

- "The trick is to get others to use your word. (To be a leader you have to have followers.)" ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 32

- "6. The Law of Exclusivity Two companies cannot own the same word in the prospect's mind." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 34

- "7. The Law of the Ladder The strategy to use depends on which rung you occupy on the ladder." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 38

- "For each category, there is a product ladder in the mind." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 39

- "Then Avis did the one thing you have to do to make progress inside the mind of the prospect. They acknowledged their position on the ladder. "Avis is only No. 2 in rent-a-cars. So why go with us? We try harder."" ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 39

- "The mind is selective. Prospects use their ladders in deciding which information to accept and which information to reject. In general, a mind accepts only new data that is consistent with its product ladder in that category. Everything else is ignored." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 40


- "How many rungs are there on your ladder? It depends on whether your product is a high-interest or a low-interest product. Products you use every day it (cigarettes, cola, beer, toothpaste, cereal) tend to be high-interest products with many rungs on their ladders.

- Products that are purchased infrequently (furniture, lawn mowers, luggage) usually have few rungs on their ladders.

- Products that involve a great deal of personal pride (automobiles, watches, cameras) are also high-interest products with many rungs on their ladders even though they are purchased infrequently." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 41

- "You tend to have twice the market share of the brand below you and half the market share of the brand above you." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 41

- "Sometimes your own ladder, or category, is too small. It might be better to be a small fish in a big pond than to be a big fish in a small pond." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 43

- "8. The Law of Duality In the long run, every market becomes a two-horse race." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 44

- "Early on, a new category is a ladder of many rungs. Gradually, the ladder becomes a two-rung affair." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 45

- "Knowing that marketing is a two-horse race in the long run can help you plan strategy in the short run." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 47

- 9. The Law of the Opposite If you're shooting for second place, your strategy is determined by the leader." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 50

- "If you want to establish a firm foothold on the second rung of the ladder, study the firm above you. Where is it strong? And how do you turn that strength into a weakness? You must discover the essence of the leader and then present the prospect with the opposite. (In other words, don't try to be better, try to be different.) It's often the upstart versus old reliable." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 51

- "When you look at customers in a given product category, there seem to be two kinds of people. There are those who want to buy from the leader and there are those who don't want to buy from the leader. A potential No. 2 has to appeal to the latter group." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 51

- "Yet, too many potential No. 2 brands try to emulate the leader. This usually is an error. You must present yourself as the alternative." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 52

- "Marketing is often a battle for legitimacy. The first brand that captures the concept is often able to portray its competitors as illegitimate pretenders." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 54

- "10. The Law of Division Over time, a category will divide and become two or more categories." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 56

- "A category starts off as a single entity […] But over time, the category breaks up into other segments. […] Like the computer, the automobile started off as a single category. Three brands (Chevrolet, Ford, and Plymouth) dominated the market. Then the category divided. Today we have luxury cars, moderately priced cars, and inexpensive cars. Full-size, intermedates, and compacts. Sports cars, four-wheel-drive vehicles, RVs, and minivans." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 57

- "Categories are dividing, not combining." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 59

- "What keeps leaders from launching a different brand to cover a new category is the fear of what will happen to their existing brands." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 61

- "Timing is also important. You can be too early to exploit a new category. […] It's better to be early than late. You can't get into the prospect's mind first unless you're prepared to spend some time waiting for things to develop." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 61

- "11. The Law of Perspective Marketing effects take place over an extended period of time." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 62

- "12. The Law of Line Extension There's an irresistible pressure to extend the equity of the brand." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 68

- "By far the most violated law in our book is the law of line extension." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 69

- "When a company becomes incredibly successful, it invariably plants the seeds for its future problems." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 69

- "In a narrow sense, line extension involves taking the brand name of a successful product (e.g., A-1 steak sauce) and putting it on a new product you plan to introduce (e.g., A-1 poultry sauce). It sounds so logical. But marketing is a battle of perception, not product. In the mind, A-1 is not the brand name, but the steak sauce itself. " ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 71

- "Invariably, the leader in any category is the brand that is not line extended." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 72

- "Less is more. If you want to be successful today, you have to narrow the focus in order to build a position in the prospect's mind." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 74

- "For a new brand to succeed, it ought to be first in a new category (chapter 1: The Law of Leadership). Or the new brand ought to be positioned as an alternative to the leader (chapter 9: The Law of the Opposite). Companies that wait until a new market has developed often find these two leadership positions already preempted. So they fall back on the old reliable line extension approach." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 75

- "13. The Law of Sacrifice You have to give up something in order to get something." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 76

- "The law of sacrifice is the opposite of the law of line extension. If you want to be successful today, you should give something up.

- There are three things to sacrifice: product line, target market, and constant change." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 77

- "Marketing is a game of mental warfare. It's a battle of perceptions, not products or services. In the mind of the prospect, Federal Express is the overnight company." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 78

- "The world of business is populated by big, highly diversified generalists and small, narrowly focused specialists." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 79

- "14. The Law of Attributes For every attribute, there is an opposite, effective attribute." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 84

- "Too often a company attempts to emulate the leader. 'They must know what works,' goes the rationale, "so let's do something similar." Not good thinking. It's much better to search for an opposite attribute that will allow you to play off against the leader. The key word here is opposite similar won't do." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 85

- "Marketing is a battle of ideas. So if you are to succeed, you must have an idea or attribute of your own to focus your efforts around." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 85

- "15. The Law of Candor When you admit a negative, the prospect will give you a positive." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 88

- "one of the most effective ways to get into a prospect's mind is to first admit a negative and then twist it into a positive." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 89

- "First and foremost, candor is very disarming. Every negative statement you make about yourself is instantly accepted as truth. Positive statements, on the other hand, are looked at as dubious at best." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 89

- "Marketing is often a search for the obvious. Since you can't change a mind once it's made up, your marketing efforts have to be devoted to using ideas and concepts already installed in the brain. You have to use your marketing programs to "rub it in."" ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 90

- "First, your "negative" must be widely perceived as a negative. It has to trigger an instant agreement with your prospect's mind. If the negative doesn't register quickly, your prospect will be confused and will wonder, "What's this all about?" Next, you have to shift quickly to the positive. The purpose of candor isn't to apologize. The candor is to set up a purpose of benefit that will convince your prospect." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 91

- "16. The Law of Singularity In each situation, only one move will produce substantial results." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 92

- "Many marketing people see success as the sum total of a lot of small efforts beautifully executed." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 93

- "Military strategist and author B.H. Liddell Hart calls this bold stroke "the line of least expectation." The Allied invasion came at Normandy, a place whose tide and rocky shore the Germans felt would be an unlikely choice for a landing of any scale." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 93

- "It's hard to find that single move if you're hanging around headquarters and not involved in the process." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 97

- "17. The Law of Unpredictability Unless you write your competitors' plans, you can't predict the future." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 98

- "Implicit in most marketing plans is an assumption about the future. Yet marketing plans based on what will happen in the future are usually wrong. […] Failure to forecast competitive reaction is a major reason for marketing failures." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 99

- "Most of corporate America's problems are not related to short-term marketing thinking. The problem is short-term financial thinking." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 99

- "Good short-term planning is coming up with that angle or word that differentiates your product or company. Then you set up a coherent long-term marketing direction that builds a program to maximize that idea or angle. It's not a long-term plan, it's a long-term direction." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 100

- "The danger in working with trends is extrapolation. Many companies jump to conclusions about how far a trend will go. […] Equally as bad as extrapolating a trend is the common practice of assuming the future will be a replay of the present." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 101

- "Research does best at measuring the past. New ideas and concepts are almost impossible to measure." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 102

- "One way to cope with an unpredictable world is to build an enormous amount of flexibility into your organization. […] but a company must be flexible enough to attack itself with a new idea. Change isn't easy, but it's the only way to cope with an unpredictable future." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 103

- "One final note that's worth mentioning: There's a difference between "predicting" the future and "taking a chance" on the future." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 103

- "18. The Law of Success Success often leads to arrogance, and arrogance to failure." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 104

- "Ego is the enemy of successful marketing. Objectivity is what's needed. When people become successful, they tend to become less objective. They often substitute their own judgment for what the market wants." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 105

- "You got into the mind first. You narrowed the focus. You preempted a powerful attribute." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 106

- "What hurts is injecting your ego in the marketing process. Brilliant marketers have the ability to think like a prospect thinks. They put themselves in the shoes of their customers. They don't impose their own view of the world on the situation." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 106

- "19. The Law of Failure Failure is to be expected and accepted." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 110

- "A better strategy is to recognize failure early and cut your losses." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 111

- "This leads to a failure to take risks. (It's hard to be first in a new category without sticking your neck out.)" ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 112

- "20. The Law of Hype The situation is often the opposite of the way it appears in the press." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 114

- "When things are going well, a company doesn't need the hype. When you need the hype, it usually means you're in trouble." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 115

- "History is filled with marketing failures that were successful in the press." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 116

- "Forget the front page. If you're looking for clues to the future, look in the back of the paper for those innocuous little stories." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 117

- "But, for the most part, hype is hype. Real revolutions don't arrive at high noon with marching bands and coverage on the 6:00 P.M. news. Real revolutions arrive unannounced in the middle of the night and kind of sneak up on you." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 119

- "21. The Law of Acceleration Successful programs are not built on fads, they're built on trends." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 120

- "A fad gets a lot of hype, and a trend gets very little. […] A fad is a short-term phenomenon that might be profitable, but a fad doesn't last long enough to do a company much good." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 121

- "Here's the paradox. If you were faced with a rapidly rising business, with all the characteristics of a fad, the best thing you could do would be to dampen the fad. By dampening the fad, you stretch the fad out and it becomes more like a trend." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 122

- "But the best, most profitable thing to ride in marketing is a long-term trend." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 123

- "22. The Law of Resources Without adequate funding an idea won't get off the ground." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 124

- "Even the best idea in the world won't go very far without the money to get it off the ground." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 125

- "Marketing is a game fought in the mind of the prospect. You need money to get into a mind. And you need money to stay in the mind once you get there." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 125

- "Remember: An idea without money is worthless. Be prepared to give away a lot for the funding." ― Al Ries, Jack Trout, The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing, p. 126
