#### Key details
- Resulting = the quality of the decision & the quality of the outcome
    - Dangerous to confuse these two elements….
- Decision making is like betting - betting on future outcome we believe is most likely to occur
- If we think like this, then we don’t need to be “right” or “wrong”, but rather what is most probable
- To make more probable decisions, we need more good-quality beliefs about ideas that are well informed and thought out.
- Once we’ve got beliefs, they are hard to change. We reinforce beliefs with motivated reasoning.
    - Aka we seek out evidence that confirms our belief and ignore or work against anything contradictory.
- What is known and to what degree of certainty
- Outcome fielding is looking at outcomes to see what we can learn from them.
- Most outcomes result from a mix of skill, luck, and unknown information
- That’s why we often make mistakes + self-service bias (where we take credit for good outcomes and blame bad ones on someone else)
- When we try to analyze other’s outcomes, we tend to do the opposite. Assigning good to luck and bad to their own mistakes.
- To be more objective about outcomes, we need better habits.
- Habits are neurological loops that have three parts: cue, routine, and reward.
- Key to changing (or making habits) is working on the routine.
- Exploring outcomes in terms of bets forces us to “truth-seek” and makes us look at things with a little more perspective. We start to explicitly see that outcomes are a mix of luck and skill.
- Others can pick apart our errors more easily than we can
- The CIA has “red-teams”, groups responsible for finding flaws in analysis and logic and arguing against conventional wisdom.
- Intellectual & ideological diversity in a group naturally produces high-quality thinking
- Temporal discounting - making decision that favour our immediate desires at the expense of our future self is something we all do
- There are a few things we can do to take care of our future selves:
    - Imagining future outcomes (backcasting)
